## Quercus Alba

My garden sits on the south side of two mature white oak trees. The main "weeds" that I encounter are the saplings of these two mother oaks. Acorns fall everywhere and are then secreted away by forgetful squirrels. A mid-succession forest garden is in constant tension with the fully grown forest, particularly if it lives along the edge of one. The saplings (not just oak, but hickory, beech, maple, and pine) would soon grow back, dwarfing and choking out most everything I've put in the ground. And so I generally pluck the saplings in their first season.

Some of the saplings I've left for multiple growing seasons; sometimes intentionally but mostly due to laziness. These toddler-trees are more resilient than their first-year siblings and are much harder to pull out of the ground, tap-root and all. This also means that they can fulfill another role in the ecosystem: Community host. I noticed this season that many of these oak saplings had strange fruit-like growths. Of course, I turned to iNaturalist[^inat-cynipini], and learned that these were "galls" created by "oak gall wasps". Here are the three that I found:

## Cyinipini

### "Oak gall wasp communities: Evolution and ecology" [^hayward2005]

My initial understanding was that the galls were a form of protection for the wasp's egg. However, the galls serve as host to a large number of other parasitoid species that may consume the original wasp larvae:

> Oak cynipid galls are microcosms of intense ecological activity, and their study holds much promise for diverse fields in evolution and ecology. They commonly contain at least three trophic levels (gall tissue; gall wasps and inquilines; parasitoids and predators), and much research has focussed on their value as model tritrophic systems. Interactions between these levels can be complex, and hyperparasitoids and superparasitoids also occur in oak gall communities.[^hayward2005]

In other words, these galls don't really protect the "galler" because there are other parasitoid species that _must_ use each specific gall to reproduce. Why then do galls exist at all if they simply provide habitat and food for other species?

> One possible explanation is that gall traits represent the ‘ghost of parasitism past’, in that they were once useful as means against parasitoid attack, but have since lost their utility due to the subsequent adaptation of parasitoids.[^hayward2005]

> It is also possible that whilst not fully excluding the majority of natural enemy species, gall traits nonetheless reduce the mortality inflicted by specific parasitoids on specific hosts. The probability of successful attack by parasitoids may be reduced in a number of ways.[^hayward2005]

There is a huge diversity in gall design strategy, indicating that there is some commensurate selection pressure in the ecosystem, likely driven by "enemy" competition. This theory posits that most of the diversity is driven by strategies to prevent or minimize the threat of parasitoid species. The main "enemy" are "inquiline" wasps that use their ovipositors to lay eggs near or even in the original larva. Here's a list of strategies mentioned in the Hayward literature review:

- Surface spines — physical barrier to ovipositor access

- Hairy / hair-covered surface — makes it hard for parasitoids to explore the surface with antennae and insert ovipositors

- Increased gall hardness — slows ovipositor drilling, raising the chance of predation or ovipositor damage

- Thick-walled parenchyma — same effect: tough tissue that drills slowly compared to soft galls

- Sticky coatings — mentioned alongside hairs as a developmental trait that may shape parasitoid access

- False chambers — decoy structures that misdirect parasitoid oviposition away from the real larval cell

- Multiple larval cells — shown to reduce parasitoid attack

- Nectar secretion to recruit ant guards — chemically/biotically mediated defense; ants attracted to extrafloral-style nectar deter parasitoids

- Phenological / size strategies (alternation of generations and rapid size growth) — not a static morphology, but a temporal strategy: galls outgrow short-ovipositor parasitoids' "windows of opportunity"

## Vocabulary

Some interesting words I didn't know while reading (not an exhaustive list):

- **tritrophic ecosystem**: a system that contains at least three trophic levels: e.g. gall tissue; gall wasps and inquilines; parasitoids and predators

- **hyperparasitoid**: specialized insects, primarily wasps (Hymenoptera), that parasitize other parasitic insects (primary parasitoids)
- **superparasitoid**: a parasite species that attacks the same host (typically an insect larva such as a caterpillar) more than once
- **apoptosis**: a form of programmed cell death that occurs in multicellular organisms and in some eukaryotic, single-celled microorganisms such as yeast
- **phytophagous**: something that eats plants
- **ectobionts**: organisms that live and feed on the external surface of another living organism (the host)
- **endobionts**: organisms that live and feeds on the internal body of another living organism (the host)
- **koinobionts**: parasitoid organisms that allow their host to continue growing, feeding, and developing after being parasitized
- **idiobionts**: parasitoid organisms that permanently paralyze or kill their host immediately upon infestation, arresting its development
- **suctorially**: of, or by, sucking
- **sympatric**: the formation of new species from a single ancestral population while inhabiting the same geographic location, occurring without physical isolation
